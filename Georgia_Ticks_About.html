Tick Ecology in the country of Georgia

An ecological analysis using ten years of surveillance data (2013–2023)

Project Overview

This project characterizes tick ecology, community composition, and biogeographic patterns across the country of Georgia using 720 tick occurrence records representing 21 species collected from 2013 to 2023. The analysis integrates occurrence data with environmental covariates to develop species distribution models, identify bioregions, and describe co-occurrence patterns among medically relevant tick species.

Key Finding: Tick diversity across Georgia is high (Shannon's H' = 2.40, Gini-Simpson = 0.89), with three distinct bioregions identified. Temperature emerged as the most influential environmental feature driving tick distribution and bioregionalization. Strong niche overlap was observed across all modeled species (mean Schoener's D = 0.91).

Key Results

Tick Diversity in Georgia

720 unique occurrence records collected

2.40 Shannon's Diversity Index (H')
0.89 Gini-Simpson diversity index

Most Frequently Recorded Species

The species with the most occurrence records were:

Ixodes ricinus — 124 records (17.22%)
Haemaphysalis punctata — 118 records (16.39%)
Dermacentor marginatus — 105 records (14.58%)

The genus Haemaphysalis was most represented with seven unique species comprising 36.8% of all occurrences.

Bioregionalization

Three distinct tick bioregions were identified using spatially constrained hierarchical clustering. The most discriminating environmental covariates between bioregions were mean temperature of the coldest quarter (F = 128.33), temperature seasonality (F = 89.69), and mean temperature of the driest quarter (F = 85.32).

Bioregion 1 (central Georgia) contained the majority of occurrence records (84.31%) and maintained the highest species richness and Shannon's Diversity.

Species Co-occurrence

Nine statistically significant positive co-occurrence relationships were detected between species pairs, with no negative associations observed. The species pair with the highest number of shared locations was D. marginatus and Ha. punctata (14 shared sites), followed by D. marginatus and Ha. parva (12 sites).

Species Distribution Models

Seven species with at least 30 presence records were modeled using boosted regression trees. Across all seven species, the southeastern portion of Georgia showed the highest predicted probability of presence.

0.91 mean Schoener's D (niche overlap)
0.74 mean Warren's I (niche overlap)

Soil organic carbon composition emerged as the most frequently important predictor, appearing among the top three variables for five of seven species. Temperature measures were also commonly ranked highly, appearing in the top predictors for all species except Ha. punctata.

About the Modeling Approach

Species distributions were modeled using a boosted regression tree (BRT) framework. Models were trained using 75% of data with 10-fold cross-validation. Pseudo-absence points were generated at least 25 km from presence locations, with equal numbers of pseudo-absence and presence points. Ten model replicates were generated per species, with the mean of predicted rasters used for finalized distribution maps.

Bioregions were identified using Ward-like hierarchical clustering with spatial constraints (ClustGeo), considering both geographical distance and environmental similarity between 25 cluster-defined sites. Co-occurrence networks were constructed using both probabilistic and count-based approaches based on site-species matrices.

Model parameters included 5,000 trees per model, interaction depth of 10, and learning rate of 0.01. Covariates with less than 1% relative influence were removed in a stepwise fashion.

Public Health Implications

This work describes the community ecology and biogeography of medically relevant tick species in Georgia. Multiple endemic species of public health importance are present, including Dermacentor reticulatus, D. marginatus, Haemaphysalis parva, H. punctata, and Ixodes ricinus.

The strong positive co-occurrence patterns and high niche overlap between vector species underscore the need for integrated surveillance and multi-species control strategies. Areas suitable for one tick vector may support multiple species, potentially increasing exposure to multiple pathogens and complicating species-specific control measures.

The predominance of temperature as a driver of tick distributions is particularly relevant under current environmental dynamics, where temperature variations may alter the suitability of regions for medically relevant species.

Disclaimer

This webpage summarizes ongoing research on tick ecology in the country of Georgia. The findings presented are based on analysis of surveillance data collected from 2013–2023 and should be interpreted as regional-level patterns. Information is not yet peer-reviewed and should not be interpreted as final or approved for dissemination.

Rarefaction analysis suggested that two to six additional tick species may be present in Georgia but were not collected in this analysis. Historical records indicate at least 29 unique species have been documented in Georgia.
